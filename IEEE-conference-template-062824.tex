\begin{abstract}
This paper details the complete custom physical implementation, rigorous
verification, and back-end parasitic extraction of a low-power, high-frequency
three-stage CMOS ring oscillator designed and laid out in a 65~nm Low-Power
(LP) CMOS process. Operating under a nominal 1.0~V supply rail, the
active core layout is manually optimized using row-based floorplanning to occupy
a compact footprint of only $73.96~\mu\text{m}^2$. Rather than relying on
idealized, unextracted schematic-level models, this work quantifies the
performance degradation imposed by back-end-of-line (BEOL)
interconnect parasitics in multi-gigahertz clocking networks. Following clean
Design Rule Checking (DRC) and Layout-Versus-Schematic (LVS) matching, a
comprehensive resistor-capacitor (RC) network extraction was performed in
Detailed Standard Parasitic Format (DSPF). Post-layout periodic steady-state
(PSS) and periodic noise (\texttt{pnoise}) simulation matrices reveal that
distributed interconnect parasitics introduce a 41.7\% reduction in loop
operating frequency, shifting the carrier baseline from an unextracted schematic
value of 11.15~GHz down to a physical limit of 6.50~GHz. At its 6.50~GHz
steady-state operating point, the extracted netlist draws an average current of
only $286.3~\mu\text{A}$, limiting total dynamic power dissipation to
286.3~$\mu$W. Spectral purity analysis indicates a single-sideband
phase noise floor of $-77.6$~dBc/Hz at a 1~MHz relative frequency offset,
yielding a normalized Figure of Merit (FoM) of
$-159.3$~dBc/Hz. To bypass post-layout simulation non-convergence issues caused
by the complex distributed netlist, a practical workaround is documented by
re-engineering the external test boundaries to match a high-impedance
1~M$\Omega$ termination floor. Overall, the results of this study
indicate that a fully verified, parasitic-aware post-layout
framework is important for accurately synthesizing and evaluating
sub-100~nm clock distribution sub-blocks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern ultra-large-scale integration (ULSI) transceivers and advanced
system-on-chip (SoC) architectures require highly area- and power-efficient
clock generation blocks, such as phase-locked loops (PLLs) and clock-and-data
recovery (CDR) subsystems \cite{razavi_analog, maneatis_dll}. Compared to
area-intensive LC-tank topologies that necessitate thick-metal analog passives,
inductorless ring oscillators offer a minimal silicon footprint, wide frequency
tuning ranges, and seamless portability across standard digital CMOS nodes
\cite{lee_cmos, pokharel_quadrature}. Among scalable ring-based configurations,
the three-stage inverter feedback loop remains a structural benchmark due to its
robust startup conditions, straightforward matching, and multi-phase output
availability \cite{nugroho_ring, maneatis_coupled}.

However, in nanometer domains like the 65~nm node utilized here, asserting
system validity solely from unextracted schematic models creates design risks.
As device dimensions scale down, intrinsic transistor capacitances drop sharply,
causing back-end-of-line (BEOL) interconnect parasitics to dominate the net node
admittance matrix \cite{hajimiri_ring}. At multi-gigahertz frequencies, routing
tracks introduce distributed parasitic resistances ($R_{\text{para}}$) and
capacitances ($C_{\text{para}}$) that form unwanted RC low-pass networks. These
networks inject additional phase shift into the feedback path, degrading loop
phase margins and pulling the physical operating frequency away from design
targets. Integrating blocks based on schematic-only metrics can therefore cause
timing mismatches.

This work addresses this modeling gap by executing the step-by-step physical
realization of a 6.5~GHz three-stage ring oscillator and explicitly isolating
the degradation penalty imposed by layout routing. The core contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \text{Custom Layout Optimization:} A manually drawn layout grouping
row-based PMOS and NMOS arrays into a compact footprint of
$73.96~\mu\text{m}^2$.
\item \text{Pre- vs. Post-Layout Quantification:} A rigorous comparative study
detailing a 41.7\% frequency reduction (from 11.15~GHz down to 6.50~GHz) and a
71.2\% inflation in single-stage propagation delay.
\item \text{CAD Convergence Workarounds:} A documented solution for periodic
steady-state (PSS) convergence failures introduced by dense extracted subcircuit
formatting.
\end{itemize}

\section{Analytical Modeling \& Circuit Architecture}
\subsection{Loop Dynamics and Startup Criteria}
The core topology is a three-stage, single-ended static CMOS inverting ring oscillator. Each stage contains a complementary PMOS pull-up and NMOS pull-down pair configured as a digital inverter, with the third stage output fed back to the first input to form the regenerative loop. To sustain autonomous periodic oscillations, the closed-loop system must satisfy the Barkhausen stability criteria at the oscillation frequency ($\omega_0$) \cite{razavi_analog}. Modeling each stage to first order as an amplifier with a low-frequency gain of $-\mathcal{A}_0$ and a dominant pole governed by node resistance ($R_{\text{node}}$) and capacitance ($C_{\text{node}}$), the single-stage transfer function is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}(s) = \frac{-\mathcal{A}_0}{1 + \frac{s}{\omega_p}}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_p = 1 / (R_{\text{node}}C_{\text{node}})$ represents the $-3\text{ dB}$ open-loop pole frequency. The total loop transfer function $\mathcal{H}_{\text{loop}}(s)$ for an $N$-stage configuration is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}_{\text{loop}}(s) = \left( \frac{-\mathcal{A}_0}{1 + \frac{s}{\omega_p}} \right)^N = \frac{(-1)^N \mathcal{A}_0^N}{\left(1 + \frac{s}{\omega_p}\right)^N}
\end{equation}

For a three-stage loop ($N=3$), the DC negative feedback contributes an explicit phase shift of $\pi$ ($180^\circ$). To achieve the total loop phase shift of $2\pi$ ($360^\circ$) necessary for regenerative instability, the three frequency-dependent poles must collectively contribute an additional phase shift of $\pi$. Assuming a symmetric design, each stage contributes an equal fraction at $\omega_0$ \cite{razavi_analog}:
\begin{equation}
\angle \mathcal{H}(\text{j}\omega_0) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\omega_0}{\omega_p}\right) = \frac{\pi}{N} = \frac{\pi}{3} = 60^\circ
\end{equation}

Evaluating (3) defines the exact relationship between the oscillation frequency $\omega_0$ and the single-stage pole frequency $\omega_p$:
\begin{equation}
\omega_0 = \omega_p \tan\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \sqrt{3}\omega_p = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{R_{\text{node}}C_{\text{node}}}
\end{equation}

Simultaneously, the loop gain magnitude must equal or exceed unity at $\omega_0$ to guarantee that initial thermal noise undergoes exponential amplification during startup \cite{lee_cmos}. Substituting (4) back into the magnitude expression defines the minimum baseline small-signal gain required per stage:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathcal{A}_0^3}{\left[\sqrt{1 + (\omega_0/\omega_p)^2}\right]^3} \ge 1 \implies \mathcal{A}_0 \ge \sqrt{1 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = 2
\end{equation}
Thus, an individual small-signal voltage gain of $\mathcal{A}_0 \ge 2$ is mathematically mandatory to sustain stable, periodic oscillations.

\subsection{Short-Channel Propagation Delay Derivation}
While small-signal parameters govern the onset of oscillations, the large-signal steady-state frequency is limited by the time-domain propagation delays of the inverters acting as non-linear switches \cite{razavi_rf}. The fundamental period ($T$) of an $N$-stage ring equals the total time required for a transitioning signal edge to traverse the entire loop twice:
\begin{equation}
T = \frac{1}{f_{\text{osc}}} = 2 N t_d
\end{equation}
where $t_d$ is the single-stage propagation delay. In classical long-channel design, $t_d$ is approximated by assuming a constant average driving current charging or discharging a single lumped capacitance across a voltage swing $\Delta V \approx V_{\text{DD}}$:
\begin{equation}
t_d \approx \frac{C_{\text{node}} V_{\text{DD}}}{2 I_{\text{drive}}}
\end{equation}

However, in deep-submicron regimes like the 65~nm Low-Power (LP) CMOS process node used here, long-channel models yield highly inaccurate estimates. Intense lateral electric fields across the ultra-short nominal gate length ($L = 60\text{ nm}$) cause carrier velocity saturation ($\nu_{\text{sat}}$) and mobility degradation to dominate device profiles \cite{razavi_analog}. Under high drain-source fields, the saturated drain current ($I_{\text{sat}}$) assumes a nearly linear dependence on the gate overdrive voltage, modeled as:
\begin{equation}
I_{\text{sat}} = \nu_{\text{sat}} C_{\text{ox}} W \left( V_{\text{GS}} - V_{\text{th}} - V_{\text{dsat}} \right)
\end{equation}
where $C_{\text{ox}}$ is the gate oxide capacitance per unit area, $W$ is the effective transistor channel width, $V_{\text{GS}}$ is the gate-source voltage, $V_{\text{th}}$ is the threshold voltage, and $V_{\text{dsat}}$ is the drain saturation voltage drop.

Accounting for short-channel current restrictions during a rail-to-rail switching transition, the propagation delay $t_d$ is formulated by integrating the time-dependent current-source constraints during the high-to-low ($t_{\text{pHL}}$) and low-to-high ($t_{\text{pLH}}$) transitions:
\begin{equation}
t_d = \frac{t_{\text{pHL}} + t_{\text{pLH}}}{2} \approx \frac{C_{\text{node}} V_{\text{DD}}}{2 \nu_{\text{sat}} C_{\text{ox}} \left[ \left(\frac{W_n+W_p}{2}\right) (V_{\text{DD}} - V_{\text{th}} - V_{\text{dsat}}) \right]}
\end{equation}

Equation (9) mathematically highlights that the physical operating frequency is strictly limited by the localized node capacitance $C_{\text{node}}$. In an unextracted schematic-level model, $C_{\text{node}}$ is exclusively composed of the intrinsic device capacitances ($C_{\text{intrinsic}} = C_{\text{gg}} + C_{\text{db}}$), representing the gate capacitance of the subsequent stage and the drain-bulk depletion capacitance of the driving stage.

\subsection{Device Sizing and Symmetrical Matching Strategy}
Symmetrical driving current capability was enforced in the TSMC 65~nm LP process
to ensure balanced rail-to-rail voltage swings. Large-signal delay components
($t_{\text{pHL}}$ and $t_{\text{pLH}}$) depend heavily on the transistor
on-resistance ($R_{\text{on}}$) and total output loading capacitance
\cite{razavi_rf}. Because hole mobility ($\mu_p$) is fundamentally lower than
electron mobility ($\mu_n$), a geometric sizing ratio of $2:1$ was locked ($W_p
= 4\text{ }\mu\text{m}$, $W_n = 2\text{ }\mu\text{m}$). Drawn physical gate
lengths are kept at the minimum $L = 60\text{ nm}$ to maximize current density,
minimize intrinsic gate charge ($Q_g$), and achieve short channel transit times
\cite{razavi_analog}. This symmetrical design balances switching transitions,
suppressing systematic duty-cycle distortion (DCD) and reducing up-converted
$1/f$ flicker noise contributions.

\subsection{Floorplanning and Interconnect Routing Topology}
The core was realized through a fully manual custom layout to preserve
high-frequency signal integrity. The topology follows an efficient two-row
floorplanning architecture: the three PMOS devices are aligned longitudinally in
an upper row within a single, shared n-well island, while the three NMOS devices
are mirrored in the lower row within the p-substrate. Grouping the PMOS devices
eliminates separate well boundary overheads and minimizes foundry spacing rules.
To suppress BEOL routing parasitics, all inter-stage loop feedback nets were
routed using short, wide tracks in the lower metallization layers (Metal 1 and
Metal 2) \cite{razavi_rf}. This bounding strategy limits via stack heights,
preventing excess parasitic resistance from dampening loop gains. The resulting
physical oscillator core footprint is $8.6\text{ }\mu\text{m} \times 8.6\text{
}\mu\text{m}$, yielding a total core area of $73.96\text{ }\mu\text{m}^2$.

\subsection{Physical Verification: Design Rule Checking (DRC)}
The manual cell layout underwent strict automated Design Rule Checking (DRC) to
verify compliance with the sub-micron lithographic rules of the 65~nm process
node. An initial Metal-1 minimum spacing violation (rule M1.S.1, min $0.09\text{
}\mu\text{m}$) discovered along the inter-stage interconnect paths was resolved
by re-adjusting track separations without expanding cell boundaries. The refined
layout achieved complete functional DRC cleanliness. Remaining open flags
reflect standard foundry layer density alerts and chip-level seal-ring rules
(CSR and AP layers). These informational items do not impact electrical
performance and are routinely addressed via automated dummy feature insertion
during final mask assembly.

\subsection{Layout-Versus-Schematic (LVS) Verification}
To guarantee that the physical geometries drawn in the layout editor map
directly onto the intended loop topology, a comprehensive
Layout-Versus-Schematic (LVS) check was executed. The LVS tool cross-examined
extracted device types, terminal pins, net connections, and transistor component
dimensions ($W/L$) against the source schematic. LVS verification yielded a
perfect match, confirming the total absence of shorts, opens, or device
mismatches. This clean baseline confirms that all subsequent performance
deviations observed during final characterization stem entirely from
interconnect physics rather than structural coding discrepancies.

\section{Parasitic-Aware Extraction \& Simulation Convergence Challenges}

\subsection{Resistor-Capacitor (RC) Netlist Extraction Matrix}
Following physical verification, back-end parasitic extraction was executed to
generate a simulation-ready network. The extraction engine analyzed active
interconnect layers, contact vias, and substrate boundaries to create an
exhaustive transistor-level netlist annotated with distributed RC elements in
Detailed Standard Parasitic Format (DSPF) \cite{razavi_rf}. 

This process isolates the parasitic loading introduced by manual routing across
the three loop nodes. As detailed in Table II, the active output node
(\texttt{Out}) exhibits the highest loading at $4.07\text{ fF}$, driven by the
added trace length required for the loop feedback path and the external testing
port. Internal nodes \texttt{net3} and \texttt{net5} display symmetrical
parasitic capacitance baselines of $3.54\text{ fF}$ and $3.52\text{ fF}$.
Additionally, the localized power rails (\texttt{Vdd} and \texttt{gnd}) carry
$3.16\text{ fF}$ and $3.01\text{ fF}$ of parasitic capacitance to the substrate.
Together, these femtofarad-scale capacitances increase the node loading,
introducing a low-pass effect within the signal loop.

\subsection{The Periodic Steady-State (PSS) Convergence Solution}
Simulating post-layout, RC-extracted oscillator netlists using Periodic
Steady-State (PSS) and periodic noise (\texttt{pnoise}) shooting algorithms
presents severe numerical convergence challenges. Initial configurations failed
repeatedly, logging non-convergence errors due to a boundary condition loading
mismatch at the output node. By default, the standard \texttt{analogLib}
simulation \texttt{port} primitive introduces a $50\text{ }\Omega$ source/load
resistance. While a $50\text{ }\Omega$ termination matches standard test
instruments, it imposes an extreme load when connected directly to an
unbuffered, low-power sub-micron CMOS inverter stage. This load severely damped
active loop gain below the Barkhausen criterion ($\mathcal{A}_0 < 2$), causing
an anomalous supply current draw of $19.59\text{ mA}$ while extinguishing the
oscillation entirely.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{sch_sim.png}
\caption{Idealized pre-layout (schematic-level) transient output waveform demonstrating an unextracted oscillation frequency of $11.15\text{ GHz}$ ($T = 89.71\text{ ps}$).}
\label{fig:sch_sim_wave}
\end{figure}

To resolve this limitation and force PSS convergence on the dense extracted
netlist, the simulation parameters were re-engineered. The internal resistance
of the output port was increased from $50\text{ }\Omega$ to a high-impedance
threshold of $1\text{ M}\Omega$, successfully removing the AC loading penalty
from the feedback loop. Symmetrical engine variables were optimized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \text{Oscillator Mode Activation:} Configured with the feedback node set explicitly to the loop output (\texttt{node+ = out\_1}), matching the target beat frequency directly to the anticipated baseline.
\item \text{Enforced Initial Conditions (IC):} An explicit voltage condition of $\text{IC} = 1.0\text{ V}$ was applied to the internal node to trigger immediate regenerative startup.
\item \text{Stabilization Interval ($t_{\text{stab}}$):} Extended to $t_{\text{stab}} = 50\text{ ns}$ to force the transient analysis to completely settle into a stable limit cycle before calculating the shooting matrix.
\item \text{Tolerance Preset:} Locked to \texttt{moderate} to balance numerical stability with multi-gigahertz Fourier matrix resolution.
\end{itemize}

For the subsequent \texttt{pnoise} analysis, the sweep range spanned $1\text{
kHz}$ to $100\text{ MHz}$ relative to the moving carrier. The noise type was
bound to \texttt{timeaverage} with noise contributions limited to phase
modulation (\texttt{PM}), and the output probe locked directly onto the
high-impedance external port (\texttt{oprobe = port}). This convergence
framework eliminated anomalous current spikes, enabling stable extraction
modeling without requiring any layout parasitic simplifications.

\section{Post-Layout Simulation Results}

\subsection{Post-Layout Transient Performance Profile}
Following PSS numerical stabilization, the post-layout transient behavior of the
RC-extracted oscillator netlist was fully characterized under a nominal
$1.0\text{ V}$ supply rail. Fig. 5 illustrates the post-layout transient
waveform, which exhibits stable rail-to-rail periodic oscillations with a
voltage swing spanning $-0.05\text{ V}$ up to $1.04\text{ V}$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{layout_sim.png}
\caption{Post-layout (RC-extracted) transient output waveform showing stable rail-to-rail oscillation at $6.50\text{ GHz}$ with a period of $153.84\text{ ps}$.}
\label{fig:layout_sim_wave}
\end{figure}

By placing adjacent peak markers ($M_1$ and $M_2$) on the settled time grid, the physical oscillation period was measured at $t_1 = 137.77\text{ ps}$ and $t_2 = 291.61\text{ ps}$, yielding an exact period of:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{post-layout}} = t_2 - t_1 = 153.84\text{ ps}
\end{equation}
The reciprocal of this period defines the true physical operating frequency of the realized oscillator core:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{osc,post-layout}} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{post-layout}}} = 6.50\text{ GHz}
\end{equation}

The dynamic supply current monitored over a full cycle via the expression \texttt{average(IT("/V0/PLUS"))} confirms a stable current draw of $|I_{\text{avg}}| = 286.3\text{ }\mu\text{A}$. The total dynamic power consumption ($P_{\text{dc}}$) evaluates to:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{dc}} = V_{\text{DD}} \times |I_{\text{avg}}| = 1.0\text{ V} \times 286.3\text{ }\mu\text{A} = 286.3\text{ }\mu\text{W}
\end{equation}
Using (6), the true large-signal propagation delay per individual inverter stage expands to $t_{\text{d,post-layout}} = T / (2N) = 25.6\text{ ps}$.

\subsection{Pre- vs. Post-Layout Performance Degradation Analysis}
Direct comparisons isolate the performance penalties imposed by physical
interconnect routing. The operating frequency drops from an unextracted
schematic baseline of $11.15\text{ GHz}$ down to a physical limit of $6.50\text{
GHz}$, a \text{41.7\% frequency reduction}. Because transistor aspects ($W/L$)
and internal current baselines remained perfectly identical across both setups,
this delay inflation is driven entirely by the distributed RC parasitics
embedded in the DSPF netlist. This parasitic load inflates the large-signal
stage delay from $14.95\text{ ps}$ up to $25.6\text{ ps}$, representing a
\text{71.2\% inflation in stage delay}. This divergence underscores that
parasitic extraction is mandatory to accurately predict timing parameters in
multi-gigahertz nanometer layouts.

\subsection{Spectral Purity and Figure of Merit Evaluation}
The phase noise spectrum of the realized oscillator was characterized using the
converged high-impedance \texttt{pnoise} simulation. As shown in Fig. 6, the
noise curve exhibits a consistent $1/f^2$ slope across the offset spectrum,
confirming that white thermal noise contributions dominate the high-frequency
phase error profile \cite{hajimiri_theory, razavi_phase}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{pnoise.png}
\caption{Post-layout (RC-extracted) single-sideband phase noise versus relative offset frequency, showing $-77.6\text{ dBc/Hz}$ at a $1\text{ MHz}$ offset.}
\label{fig:pnoise_plot}
\end{figure}

At a relative offset of $\Delta f = 1\text{ MHz}$ away from the $6.50\text{ GHz}$ carrier, the oscillator achieves a phase noise baseline of $-77.6\text{ dBc/Hz}$. At an offset of $\Delta f = 100\text{ MHz}$, the noise floor drops cleanly to $-124.3\text{ dBc/Hz}$. To evaluate performance balancing across frequency, power, and spectral purity, metrics were normalized using the standard IEEE oscillator Figure of Merit (FoM) formulation \cite{lee_tutorial}:
\begin{equation}
\text{FoM} = \mathcal{L}(\Delta f) - 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{f_{\text{osc}}}{\Delta f}\right) + 10\log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{dc}}}{1\text{ mW}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)$ represents the single-sideband phase noise. Substituting the extracted post-layout metrics ($\mathcal{L}(1\text{ MHz}) = -77.6\text{ dBc/Hz}$, $f_{\text{osc}} = 6.50\text{ GHz}$, and $P_{\text{dc}} = 286.3\text{ }\mu\text{W}$) into (13) provides the following evaluation:
\begin{align}
\text{FoM} &= -77.6 - 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{6.5\times 10^9}{1.0\times 10^6}\right) + 10\log_{10}\left(0.2863\right) \nonumber \\
&= -77.6 - 76.26 - 5.43 = -159.3\text{ dBc/Hz}
\end{align}
An FoM of $-159.3\text{ dBc/Hz}$ confirms that the compact, manually optimized physical design maintains an efficient balance between power consumption and spectral purity within the 65~nm node. It should be noted that this favorable FoM is aided by the sub-milliwatt power consumption; the absolute phase noise of $-77.6\text{ dBc/Hz}$ at $1\text{ MHz}$ is typical of an unoptimized single-ended ring oscillator and leaves room for improvement through established low-noise delay-cell techniques.

\subsection{Qualitative Context with Prior Art}
A direct quantitative comparison with prior ring oscillators is not attempted
here, since most reported designs are measured, frequency-tunable oscillators,
whereas this work characterizes a fixed-frequency core through post-layout
simulation. Reported CMOS ring oscillators span a wide range of technology nodes
and tuning strategies, including current-controlled and quadrature topologies in
0.18~$\mu$m CMOS~\cite{nugroho_ring, pokharel_quadrature}, injection-based
designs for all-digital PLLs~\cite{edge_injection_ref13}, and current-starved
ring VCOs targeting 5G bands~\cite{vco_65nm_ref14}. Relative to these, the
present design is distinguished less by raw performance than by the fully
verified, parasitic-aware methodology used to obtain its figures; the compact
$73.96~\mu\text{m}^2$ core and $286.3~\mu$W power consumption are reported as
simulated post-layout values rather than measured silicon.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented the custom physical design, layout realization, and
post-layout verification of a low-power three-stage CMOS ring oscillator in a
65~nm LP CMOS baseline. By executing a manual cell floorplanning strategy, the
core layout area was optimized down to a compact footprint of
73.96~$\mu\text{m}^2$. Under a nominal 1.0~V supply rail, the fully extracted RC
netlist achieved stable physical oscillations at 6.50~GHz while restricting core
power consumption to 286.3~$\mu$W. Periodic noise simulations verified a
single-sideband phase noise baseline of $-77.6$~dBc/Hz at a 1~MHz offset from
the carrier, evaluating to a competitive normalized FoM of $-159.3$~dBc/Hz.

A pre- and post-layout comparison quantified a 41.7\% frequency reduction from
the idealized schematic baseline of 11.15~GHz, illustrating how strongly BEOL
routing parasitics degrade deep-submicron clock trees. This performance shift
corresponds to a 71.2\% inflation in the single-stage propagation delay
(increasing from 14.95~ps up to 25.6~ps), indicating that schematic-level
estimates can substantially overstate achievable performance in nanometer
regimes. To resolve periodic steady-state (PSS) CAD convergence failures
introduced by the dense extracted netlist, the output boundary condition was
re-engineered by matching the external test port to a high-impedance 1~M$\Omega$
floor. Overall, the results indicate that a fully verified, parasitic-aware
implementation framework is important for synthesizing multi-gigahertz clock
generation blocks in modern system-on-chip architectures.

The present results are based on post-layout, parasitic-extracted simulation;
silicon measurement is left as future work. Future extensions also include
adding explicit frequency tuning and applying low-noise delay-cell techniques to
improve the absolute phase noise.
